# Hawaii Building Code - Key Amendments

**Based on**: Hawaii State Building Code (HBC), adopting IBC 2018
**Base Code**: IBC 2018 with Hawaii amendments; each county has its own adopted code and local amendments
**Authority**: Hawaii Department of Accounting and General Services (DAGS) for state-owned buildings; County building departments govern all private construction — Honolulu (DPP), Maui, Hawaii (Big Island), and Kauai counties each have independent authority

## Energy Code

**Adopted**: Hawaii Energy Conservation Code (HECC), based on IECC 2018 with Hawaii amendments

**Climate Zone**: 1A (tropical/humid) — all islands

**Zone 1A Residential Envelope Minimums (IECC 2018)**:
- Ceiling/attic: R-19 (much less than mainland; cooling dominated)
- Wood-frame walls: R-13
- Floors over unconditioned space: R-0 (not required for elevated floors with tropical ventilation)
- Slab edge: no requirement for Zone 1A
- Windows: U-0.50, SHGC 0.25 (SHGC is critical — solar heat gain reduction is primary driver)

**Hawaii-Specific Energy Design Priorities**:
- **Cooling dominated**: Unlike all mainland climates, there are essentially zero heating loads on all islands
- **SHGC 0.25**: Solar heat gain coefficient is the most critical window performance value; reducing solar heat gain drives cooling energy savings
- **Natural ventilation**: Trade winds (predominantly from the northeast) allow passive cooling for much of the year; building orientation and cross-ventilation design significantly reduce or eliminate air conditioning energy in well-designed homes
- **Mechanical cooling**: Air conditioning is commonly installed but can often be reduced or eliminated with proper passive design
- **Solar PV**: Hawaii has the highest residential solar adoption rate in the nation (over 50% of homes); PV payback periods are short due to the highest electricity rates in the US
- **Net metering**: Hawaii utility regulators have adjusted net metering programs due to grid saturation; battery storage is now commonly paired with PV systems
- **Hawaii Clean Energy Initiative**: State has committed to 100% renewable electricity by 2045; strong incentive programs for solar, battery storage, and heat pump water heaters

**Trade Wind Cooling Design**:
- Orient building to capture northeast trade winds
- Cross-ventilation: place openings on both windward (northeast) and leeward (southwest) sides
- Louvered or jalousie windows: traditional Hawaii residential element that maximizes ventilation while maintaining weatherproofing
- Covered lanais: shade primary glazing from direct sun while allowing airflow
- Roof overhangs: wide overhangs reduce solar gain on walls and windows; standard Hawaii vernacular element

## Residential Code

**Adopted**: IRC 2018 base; each county adopts its own version with local amendments

**County Code Adoption Status**:
| County | Primary Code | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Honolulu (Oahu) | IBC/IRC 2018 with local amendments | Most stringent; own DPP code |
| Maui County | IBC/IRC 2018 | Covers Maui, Molokai, and Lanai |
| Hawaii County (Big Island) | IBC/IRC 2018 | Lava zone requirements apply |
| Kauai County | IBC/IRC 2018 | High hurricane history (Iniki 1992) |

**Residential Sprinklers by County**:
- **Honolulu County**: Residential fire sprinklers required for new one- and two-family dwellings
- **Maui County**: Verify with Maui DPW; requirements vary by project type
- **Hawaii County**: Verify with Hawaii County Building Division
- **Kauai County**: Verify with Kauai County Planning Department
- Always confirm sprinkler requirements with the county building department at project kickoff

## Seismic / Wind / Natural Hazards

### Seismic

Hawaii is an active volcanic island chain sitting above a mantle hotspot. The islands are seismically active with frequent earthquakes, particularly on the Big Island.

| Island | Typical SDC | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Hawaii (Big Island) | C-D | Active volcanic activity; frequent M5.0-7.0+ earthquakes |
| Maui | B-C | Moderate seismicity |
| Oahu | B-C | Honolulu area; moderate |
| Kauai | B-C | Moderate seismicity |

**Big Island Seismicity**:
- M6.0+ earthquakes occur regularly; associated with volcanic activity and oceanic plate flexure
- 2018 Kilauea Lower East Rift Zone eruption: destroyed over 700 homes in Leilani Estates and Lanipuna Gardens subdivision
- 2006 Kiholo Bay earthquake (M6.7) caused significant damage across the Big Island and on Oahu
- Structural engineering recommended even for residential projects in higher SDC areas

**Seismic Design Requirements**:
- SDC C: Intermediate seismic detailing; engineered anchor bolts; braced wall panels per IRC with SDC C provisions
- SDC D: Enhanced detailing; may require structural engineering for complex residential projects

### Tsunami - Critical for All Hawaii Coastal Areas

**All islands and all coastal areas in Hawaii face tsunami risk. This is one of the most important site selection and design considerations for any Hawaii project.**

**Historical Tsunamis**:
- 1946 Aleutian tsunami: Hilo runup exceeded 30 ft; destroyed much of downtown Hilo; 159 killed in Hawaii
- 1960 Chilean earthquake tsunami: Hilo runup 35 ft; 61 killed in Hawaii; city rebuilt inland
- 1964 Alaska earthquake tsunami: Affected all islands
- 2011 Japan earthquake tsunami: Reached Hawaii; caused significant damage in some harbors

**Tsunami Design Requirements**:
- ASCE 7 Chapter 6 (Tsunami Loads and Effects): applies to Risk Category II, III, and IV structures in designated tsunami design zones; residential Risk Category II structures in Zone T1 and higher require compliance
- Hawaii Emergency Management Agency (HI-EMA) and NOAA publish tsunami inundation maps for all major communities on all islands; site location relative to inundation zones must be evaluated before design begins
- Tsunami evacuation zones: Zone 1 (highest risk, nearest coast) through Zone 5 (lowest risk); check zone for every coastal project site
- **Hilo, Hawaii County**: Entire bayfront area is in high tsunami hazard zone; many restrictions on development in lowest zone

**Design Approaches for Tsunami Zones**:
- Preferred: site building outside inundation zone
- If within zone: elevate lowest habitable floor above inundation elevation (HI-EMA provides design flood elevations for tsunami)
- Structural open foundation: allow water to flow through below habitable floor level (breakaway walls or open piles)
- Tsunami-resistant construction: reinforced concrete for structures required to remain operational post-tsunami

### Hurricane and Tropical Cyclone

**Hawaii is subject to tropical cyclones (hurricanes and tropical storms) originating in the Central Pacific. Hurricane Iniki (1992) struck Kauai directly as a Category 4 storm and remains the costliest natural disaster in Hawaii history.**

| Island | Basic Wind Speed (Vult) | Notes |
|--------|------------------------|-------|
| Kauai | 130-140 mph | Direct hurricane track history; highest risk |
| Oahu | 115-120 mph | Honolulu; some orographic protection |
| Maui | 115-125 mph | Variable; windward/leeward exposure |
| Big Island | 100-115 mph | Orographic protection from Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa for most areas |

**Hurricane Design Requirements**:
- ASCE 7-16 wind design for all Hawaii projects
- Impact-resistant glazing or approved hurricane shutters: required on all islands for windows and glazed openings; wind-borne debris design per ASCE 7
- Continuous load path: roof-to-wall-to-foundation connections; hurricane straps at every rafter or truss
- Product approval: windows, doors, and skylights require product approval documentation showing compliance with design wind pressure
- Roof-to-wall connections: all roofing products must be rated for local design wind speed
- Garage doors: wind pressure rated; multiple point locking systems

**Trade Wind vs. Hurricane Design**:
- Trade winds are predominantly from the northeast at 15-20 mph sustained; not a structural concern but drive passive cooling design
- Hurricane design is the structural wind requirement; uses design wind speed from ASCE 7 wind hazard maps

### Lava Zones - Big Island Only

The Big Island of Hawaii has nine lava hazard zones mapped by USGS Hawaiian Volcano Observatory based on historical eruption frequency and proximity to active rift zones.

| Zone | Risk | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| Zone 1 | Highest | Active rift zones; eruption sites; no viable development |
| Zone 2 | Very High | Adjacent to Zone 1; historically active lava flows within Zone 2 since 1800 |
| Zone 3 | High | Includes areas within Ka'u and Puna districts; less frequent flows |
| Zone 4 | Moderate-High | Southeast Mauna Loa; flows every few hundred years |
| Zone 5 | Moderate | North Kona Coast and Kohala; moderate volcanic risk |
| Zone 6 | Low-Moderate | Older lava fields; less frequent eruption history |
| Zones 7-9 | Low | Oldest flows; lowest volcanic risk |

**Lava Zone Implications**:
- **Zone 1**: Development essentially prohibited in active flow areas; no reasonable building code mitigation for active lava
- **Zone 2**: Insurance extremely difficult or impossible to obtain at any reasonable cost; conventional mortgage financing unavailable or very expensive; cash buyers only in most cases
- **Zone 3**: Insurance available but at significant premium; financing available with caveats; many lenders require special hazard insurance riders
- **2018 Eruption lesson**: Leilani Estates (primarily Zone 1-2) was inundated by lava; over 700 structures destroyed; some Zone 2-3 boundary areas were also affected
- **Building code implications**: No specific building design can provide meaningful protection from active lava flow; the lava zone classification affects permitting, insurance, and financing but is primarily a land use and financial risk issue rather than a structural design mitigation opportunity
- **County permit process**: Hawaii County Building Division checks lava zone before issuing permits; some zones require additional disclosure to buyers

### Volcanic Emissions - Vog

**Vog (volcanic air pollution or volcanic smog) is a unique Hawaii environmental condition caused by sulfur dioxide (SO2) and particulate emissions from Kilauea volcano (and occasionally Mauna Loa) on the Big Island.**

- **Source**: Kilauea summit and lower East Rift Zone eruptions release SO2 that reacts with oxygen, water vapor, and sunlight to form sulfuric acid mist and particulate
- **Distribution**: Trade winds carry vog southwest and west; Kona Coast and Ka'u district on Big Island most affected; Kona winds occasionally push vog to other islands (Oahu, Maui, Kauai can all receive vog)
- **Health effects**: Respiratory irritant; particularly harmful to people with asthma, heart disease, and elderly; not a construction code matter but affects occupant health
- **Corrosion effects**: Sulfuric acid mist accelerates corrosion of metal, concrete, and stone surfaces; particularly aggressive to exposed metal fasteners, electrical conduit, HVAC equipment, and roofing metals on the Big Island Kona and Ka'u districts
- **Design response for vog-affected areas**:
  - Specify corrosion-resistant fasteners and connectors (stainless steel or hot-dipped galvanized) for all exposed applications
  - HVAC equipment with corrosion-resistant coated coils
  - Sealed electrical conduit
  - Consider indoor air filtration for sensitive occupants
  - Concrete and masonry: use sulfate-resistant cement formulations

### Termites - Mandatory Treatment Statewide

**Hawaii has the most severe termite problem in the United States. Both Formosan subterranean termites (the most destructive termite species in the world) and drywood termites are present statewide.**

**Termite Treatment Requirements**:
- Pre-construction soil treatment: required for all new residential construction; applied to soil and foundation forms before slab pour or after backfilling
- All wood in contact with soil or concrete must be pressure-treated or naturally termite-resistant (California redwood heartwood, teak, certain other species)
- Structural framing: pressure-treated lumber (minimum ACQ or copper azole treatment) for all framing within 18" of grade; many Hawaii builders treat all framing
- Concrete slab construction: physical soil treatment barrier plus periodic re-treatment program
- Steel frame construction: eliminates subterranean termite threat to framing but drywood termites can still infest wood sheathing, trim, and finishes
- Post-construction treatment: periodic termite inspection and re-treatment recommended; annual inspection standard practice
- Hawaii Fumigation: drywood termite treatment for existing structures typically requires whole-building tenting (fumigation); new construction should be designed to minimize future fumigation disruption

**Wood Species Considerations**:
- Naturally termite-resistant species (appropriate for Hawaii): teak, ipe, black locust, some cedars
- Pressure-treated lumber: minimum UC4B for ground contact; UC3B for above-grade exterior
- Composite and fiber cement: not susceptible to termite damage; widely used in Hawaii for trim and siding

### Corrosion-Resistant Construction

**Marine Environment**:
- All islands are surrounded by ocean; salt air affects all coastal locations
- Corrosion-resistant construction is required in coastal areas and strongly recommended statewide given Hawaii's island geography
- Hawaii County and other jurisdictions have explicit corrosion-resistance requirements for structural connectors and fasteners

**Fasteners and Connectors**:
- Exterior applications: stainless steel (Type 304 or 316) or hot-dipped galvanized (HDG) minimum
- Within 1,000 ft of ocean: Type 316 stainless steel recommended
- Structural connectors: code-required use of approved corrosion-resistant connectors
- Avoid electroplated zinc (not adequate) and aluminum in contact with concrete

**HVAC and MEP Equipment**:
- Coastal-rated HVAC units with corrosion-resistant coils (copper or epoxy-coated aluminum)
- Stainless steel ductwork in corrosive environments
- Plumbing: copper or CPVC; avoid uncoated galvanized in coastal areas

## Notable State Amendments

1. **County-by-county code adoption**: Hawaii has no single statewide building code for private construction; each of the four counties (Honolulu, Maui, Hawaii, Kauai) adopts and amends codes independently; always verify the specific edition and amendments in effect with the county building department
2. **Residential sprinklers**: Honolulu County requires sprinklers in new single-family homes; other counties have varying requirements; verify at project kickoff
3. **Tsunami design**: All coastal projects must evaluate tsunami inundation maps; ASCE 7 Chapter 6 compliance required for structures in designated tsunami design zones
4. **Lava zone**: Hawaii County checks and discloses lava zone at permit application; Zones 1-2 have significant permitting and insurance complications
5. **Termite treatment**: Mandatory statewide; specific products and application methods specified by county; contractor licensing required
6. **Special Management Area (SMA)**: Coastal development within the SMA (typically within several hundred feet of the shoreline) requires a SMA permit under Hawaii Revised Statutes Chapter 205A in addition to building permit; applies to all counties; administered by each county planning department
7. **Agricultural land protections**: Significant portions of all islands are zoned agricultural (A-1 or A-2 districts); residential development on agricultural land is severely restricted; verify zoning before committing to site design
8. **Historic preservation**: State Historic Preservation Division (SHPD) review required for projects that may affect historic or archaeological resources; significant concern on all islands given Hawaiian historical sites

## Quick Checklist - New Residential

- [ ] County confirmed; verify adopted code edition and local amendments with county building department
- [ ] Tsunami inundation map reviewed (HI-EMA maps); site location relative to tsunami evacuation zones documented
- [ ] Lava zone confirmed (Big Island projects); Zone 1-2 financing and insurance implications discussed with owner
- [ ] Hurricane design wind speed (Vult) determined per ASCE 7-16; Kauai 130-140 mph, other islands 100-125 mph
- [ ] Impact-resistant glazing or approved hurricane shutters specified for all windows and glazed openings
- [ ] Continuous load path: hurricane straps at every rafter or truss; roof-to-wall-to-foundation connection designed
- [ ] SHGC 0.25 or lower windows specified; solar heat gain control is primary window performance driver
- [ ] Building orientation: capture northeast trade winds for passive cooling; cross-ventilation openings both windward and leeward
- [ ] Wide roof overhangs designed to shade primary glazing from direct sun
- [ ] Termite pre-treatment: soil treatment required; treated lumber at foundation and within 18" of grade; whole-structure treatment scope confirmed
- [ ] All framing lumber pressure-treated or naturally resistant species
- [ ] Corrosion-resistant fasteners: stainless steel or hot-dipped galvanized for exterior and structural connections
- [ ] Vog-affected areas (Big Island Kona/Ka'u): corrosion-resistant HVAC coils; sealed conduit; sulfate-resistant concrete
- [ ] Residential sprinkler requirement: check with county building department (Honolulu: required)
- [ ] Seismic design category (SDC) confirmed; Big Island SDC C-D; structural detailing required
- [ ] SMA permit required? Verify coastal setback and Special Management Area boundaries with county planning
- [ ] Agricultural zoning: confirm residential use is permitted on project parcel
- [ ] Rooftop PV solar: rough-in conduit and panel capacity at minimum; full system sizing discussion with owner
- [ ] HVAC: cooling-dominated climate; consider heat pump water heater
- [ ] Smoke alarms per IRC 2018 in all required locations
- [ ] Egress windows per IRC R310 in all sleeping rooms
