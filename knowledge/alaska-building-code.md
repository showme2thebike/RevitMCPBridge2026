**Based on**: Alaska Building Code, adopting IBC 2018 with extensive Alaska amendments

**Authority**: Alaska Division of Fire and Life Safety (Department of Public Safety); Municipality of Anchorage (MOA), Fairbanks North Star Borough (FNSB), and other jurisdictions have independent building departments

## Energy Code

**Adopted**: IECC 2018 with Alaska amendments

**Climate Zones**:
| Region | Climate Zone | Cities / Areas |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Southeast AK (panhandle) | 6A-7 | Juneau, Ketchikan, Sitka |
| Southcentral AK | 7 | Anchorage, Kenai Peninsula, Kodiak |
| Interior AK | 7-8 | Fairbanks, Delta Junction, Tok |
| Western AK | 7-8 | Nome, Bethel, Kotzebue |
| Arctic / North Slope | 8 | Barrow/Utqiagvik, Prudhoe Bay, Deadhorse |

**Zone 7 Residential Envelope Minimums (IECC 2018)**:
- Ceiling/attic: R-49
- Wood-frame walls: R-20 + R-5ci or R-13 + R-10ci
- Mass walls: R-11.4/R-16.4
- Floors over unconditioned space: R-38
- Slab edge: R-15 for 4 ft
- Windows: U-0.28, SHGC no requirement

**Zone 8 Residential Envelope Minimums (IECC 2018)**:
- Ceiling/attic: R-49 (many projects exceed R-60 for long-term durability)
- Wood-frame walls: R-20 + R-5ci minimum; best practice R-30+ with exterior continuous insulation
- Floors over unconditioned or exterior-adjacent space: R-38 minimum; over permafrost see permafrost section
- Slab edge: R-15 for 4 ft
- Windows: U-0.25 or better; triple-pane standard in Zone 8

**Alaska-Specific Energy Considerations**:
- Alaska Housing Finance Corporation (AHFC) energy ratings are widely used for financing and energy assistance program eligibility; many projects are designed to AHFC criteria, not just IECC minimum
- Blower door target: IECC requires 3.0 ACH50; many Zone 8 projects target 1.5 ACH50 or tighter
- Triple-pane windows with low-emissivity coatings: essentially mandatory in Zone 8 despite U-0.25 code minimum; many projects target U-0.18 or better
- Heat Recovery Ventilator (HRV): required practice in all tight Zone 7-8 buildings for moisture control and indoor air quality
- Heating design temperatures: Anchorage -18F; Fairbanks -47F; Barrow -43F; Nome -27F; Bethel -36F; Kodiak 10F

**Vapor and Moisture Control in Zone 7-8**:
- Class I vapor retarder (6-mil polyethylene film) on warm-in-winter side of framing
- Continuous air barrier is equally or more critical than vapor retarder in extreme cold climates
- Avoid double vapor barrier configurations (moisture trap between two impermeable layers)
- All envelope penetrations must be sealed — electrical boxes, plumbing, mechanical
- Interior humidity management: HRV or ERV prevents moisture accumulation in tight homes

## Residential Code

**Adopted**: IRC 2018 with Alaska amendments

**Key IRC Provisions for Alaska**:
- Minimum ceiling height: 7'-0" habitable space
- Egress windows: 5.7 sq ft net clear per IRC R310
- Smoke alarms: each sleeping room, outside sleeping area, each story
- Carbon monoxide alarms: required; especially critical given widespread fossil fuel heating dependence
- Garage separation: 1/2" gypsum minimum on dwelling side

**Residential Sprinklers**:
- No statewide mandate for one- and two-family dwellings
- Municipality of Anchorage: verify current local requirements
- Rural Alaska: long fire department response times (hours or days in remote communities) make sprinklers strongly advisable as a life-safety measure even where not required

## Seismic - Alaska Has Among the Highest Seismic Risk in the United States

### Tectonic Setting

Alaska sits on the Pacific Ring of Fire and is subject to multiple active plate boundaries:

- **Aleutian Subduction Zone**: Pacific Plate subducting under North American Plate. Generates magnitude 8.0-9.5+ megathrust earthquakes. The 1964 Great Alaska Earthquake (Mw 9.2) is the largest earthquake in US recorded history and the second largest ever recorded globally. It permanently shaped Alaska building codes and land use policies.
- **Queen Charlotte-Fairweather Fault System**: Major strike-slip fault along the SE Alaska panhandle and into British Columbia
- **Denali Fault System**: Interior strike-slip fault system; the 2002 Denali Fault earthquake was Mw 7.9
- **Castle Mountain Fault**: Near Anchorage and the MatSu Valley; capable of generating M7.0+

### Seismic Design Categories

| Region | Typical SDC | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Southcentral AK (Anchorage) | D-E | High seismic; liquefaction risk in coastal and floodplain areas |
| Interior AK (Fairbanks) | C-D | Denali Fault proximity |
| Southeast AK (Juneau, Sitka) | D | Fairweather/Queen Charlotte system |
| Western AK / Aleutians | D-E | Active subduction zone; volcanic hazard |
| North Slope / Barrow | C | Lower but not negligible seismicity |

**SDC D-E Structural Requirements for Residential**:
- IRC prescriptive shear wall and anchor tables are NOT applicable in SDC D-E; structural engineering is required
- Special wood-frame shear walls with rated sheathing panels and engineered nailing schedules
- Hold-downs at shear wall ends
- Anchor bolts per engineered design (not IRC prescriptive Table R403.1.6)
- Engineered foundation design required
- Special inspection typically required for shear walls and anchor systems

### Site-Specific Seismic Concerns

**Liquefaction Risk**:
- Anchorage coastal areas, river deltas, tide flat fills, and estuarine deposits: high liquefaction potential during a major seismic event
- The 1964 earthquake caused massive liquefaction-induced landslides in Anchorage (Turnagain Heights neighborhood destroyed; L Street Slide)
- Geotechnical investigation is required for sites with saturated loose granular soils, soft clays, or artificial fills

**Tsunamis**:
- All Alaska coastal areas lie in tsunami hazard zones
- Alaska Tsunami Warning Center (ATWC) provides earthquake-triggered tsunami alerts
- NOAA and Alaska Division of Community and Regional Affairs (DCRA) publish tsunami inundation maps for populated coastal communities
- ASCE 7 Chapter 6 tsunami provisions apply to Risk Category III and IV structures; all coastal residential projects must evaluate site location on available inundation maps
- Design considerations: habitable floor elevation relative to inundation zone; evacuation route access from the site

**Seismically Triggered Landslides**:
- Common in mountainous, glaciated, and coastal bluff terrain
- Site assessment required for any hillside or bluff-edge development

**Active Volcanoes**:
- Cook Inlet volcanoes (Spurr, Redoubt, Iliamna, Augustine) periodically produce ash fall affecting Anchorage and Kenai Peninsula
- Ash fall considerations: HVAC filter management, structural loading from wet ash accumulation, corrosion of exposed metal

## Permafrost - Alaska's Unique Foundation Challenge

Permafrost affects approximately 80% of Alaska's land area and is one of the most critical design considerations for any Alaska building project outside Anchorage and the panhandle.

### Types of Permafrost

| Type | Description | Location |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Continuous permafrost | Ground permanently frozen year-round | Arctic; most of interior north of Fairbanks |
| Discontinuous permafrost | Frozen in patches; thaw areas possible | Fairbanks area, western AK, parts of southcentral |
| Sporadic permafrost | Isolated frozen areas | Parts of southcentral AK; south of discontinuous zone |
| Permafrost-free | No permanently frozen ground | Anchorage, SE AK, most of Kenai Peninsula |

**Active Layer**: The surface layer that freezes and thaws seasonally. Thickness ranges from 1-2 ft in the Arctic to 3-6 ft in the discontinuous zone. Building heat must never be allowed to penetrate the active layer into permafrost intended as structural support.

### Foundation Approaches in Permafrost Zones

**Strategy 1: Preserve the Permafrost (Preferred in Continuous Permafrost Zone)**
- Elevate the building on open piles or piers to allow cold air circulation in the space below the floor
- Maintain the frozen ground as the structural medium
- Pile types: driven steel pipe piles, helical piles, auger-cast piles embedded into permafrost below the active layer
- Pile embedment: typically 10-20 ft into permafrost, depending on soil bearing capacity and active layer depth
- Thermosyphons (heat pipes): passive refrigeration devices installed alongside piles to extract heat from the ground and maintain frozen soil temperatures; no moving parts, no energy input required
- Ventilated underfloor space: open perimeter allows cold air circulation; floor insulation R-40 minimum

**Strategy 2: Thaw and Stabilize (Limited Use)**
- Pre-thaw and compact the soil before construction
- Used only where permafrost has high ice content and some thaw is unavoidable
- Risk of differential settlement if thaw is non-uniform over the building footprint
- Rarely preferred for new residential construction due to long-term performance uncertainty

**Strategy 3: Insulated Gravel Pad**
- Thick gravel pad (4-8 ft+) provides a thermal buffer
- Insulation board placed at the pad-to-building interface
- More commonly used for roads and large equipment pads
- For heated buildings: heat output eventually may thaw ground beneath pad over service life; thermosyphons sometimes added as backup

**Key Permafrost Design Rules**:
- Never introduce sustained heat to permafrost that you intend to remain frozen and structural
- Utility connections (water and sewer) must be insulated utilidors or continuously heat-traced lines; buried pipes will freeze or thaw permafrost
- Floor insulation: R-40 minimum over permafrost-sensitive foundations
- Post-construction monitoring: annual inspection of pile heads and floor levelness for differential settlement
- Climate change: permafrost thaw rates are accelerating statewide; design service life assumptions must account for potential future degradation

**Permafrost Geotechnical Investigation Requirements**:
- Geotechnical investigation is mandatory for any structure on discontinuous or continuous permafrost
- Borings or test pits to characterize active layer depth, permafrost depth, ground ice content
- Thermal regime analysis: seasonal temperature profiles at proposed foundation depth
- Municipal building departments (Fairbanks, Nome, Bethel) specify minimum geotech scope

## Snow Loads - Highest in the Nation

Alaska receives the highest snow loads in the United States. Roof structural design for snow is typically the controlling load combination in most of the state.

| Region | Ground Snow Load (Pg) |
|--------|----------------------|
| Anchorage | 50-70 psf |
| Kenai / Homer | 50-100 psf (high coastal moisture) |
| Kodiak Island | 60-100+ psf |
| Juneau / SE AK | 60-100+ psf |
| Fairbanks | 30-50 psf (drier interior; less snow but extreme cold) |
| Nome | 40-60 psf |
| Barrow/Utqiagvik | 20-30 psf (arctic desert; less total snow but high wind redistribution) |
| Valdez | 150-200 psf (world-class snowfall area) |
| Summit Pass / Thompson Pass area | 200-400+ psf (site-specific snow study required) |

**Critical Snow Design Issues**:
- Site-specific snow studies required for complex terrain or extreme precipitation areas (Valdez, Thompson Pass, any mountain pass project)
- Drift accumulation at roof step changes and adjacent to parapets and walls: ASCE 7 Chapter 7 drift analysis required
- Sliding snow from steep metal roofs: design and document slide zones at grade; pedestrian injury hazard must be managed
- Roof access and snow removal: structural design must accommodate personnel loading during manual snow clearing operations
- Flat or low-slope roofs in high-snowfall areas: operational snow removal plan may be required; structural design for accumulated snow between removal events
- Heated residential buildings: unbalanced loads on gable roofs per ASCE 7 Chapter 7

## Wind Design

| Region | Basic Wind Speed (Vult) | Notes |
|--------|------------------------|-------|
| Interior AK (Fairbanks) | 90-100 mph | Sheltered river valley; lower than coastal |
| Anchorage / Cook Inlet | 100-115 mph | Cook Inlet gap winds can be extreme; Turnagain Arm |
| SE AK panhandle | 100-120 mph | Southeast storm tracks; gap winds through coastal fjords |
| Kodiak / Alaska Peninsula | 110-130 mph | Extreme coastal exposure; frequent Williwaw events |
| Aleutian Islands | 120-150+ mph | Most extreme sustained wind exposure in US territory |
| North Slope coastal | 100-120 mph | Arctic storms; blizzard conditions common |

**Williwaw Winds**:
- Sudden, violent katabatic downslope wind events that are common in mountain-adjacent coastal areas (Kodiak, Dutch Harbor, Valdez, SE Alaska fjords)
- Can exceed design wind speeds without warning; episodic and severe
- Buildings in susceptible terrain: enhanced cladding anchorage, roof-to-wall connections, and tie-down systems

## Avalanche Zones

Avalanche is a significant life-safety hazard in Alaska mountain terrain. Evaluation is required for any project in or near potential runout zones.

- **Design Standards**: American Avalanche Association guidelines; ASCE 7 snow and wind loads inform structural impact analysis for avalanche-resistant construction
- **Hazard Mapping**: Alaska Division of Geological and Geophysical Surveys (DGGS) provides avalanche hazard maps for many communities; site-specific avalanche study required for mountain terrain development projects
- **High-Risk Locations**: Juneau (multiple fatal avalanche events affecting occupied structures), Valdez, Haines, Skagway, Seward, Whittier, and any coastal or mountain community where terrain allows debris flow
- **Juneau specifics**: City has formal avalanche hazard mapping; development in mapped hazard zones is restricted or requires engineering analysis and approval
- **Design Options (in order of preference)**:
  1. Avoidance: site the building outside the mapped runout zone (strongly preferred)
  2. Deflection: structural deflection walls uphill of building to redirect debris
  3. Resistance: reinforced concrete or earth-sheltered construction designed to resist debris impact load

## Unique Alaska Conditions

### Remote and Rural Construction

- **Fly-in communities**: Construction materials arrive by small aircraft or seasonal barge; heavy equipment unavailable in many villages; structural system selection must reflect material delivery and handling constraints
- **Log construction**: Traditional and widely used throughout rural Alaska; governed by IRC Chapter 10; local contractor experience with log construction critical to successful outcomes
- **Prefabricated and panelized systems**: Common for cost control and quality assurance in remote areas; panels fabricated in Anchorage or Fairbanks and flown or barged to site
- **Indigenous construction**: Traditional building forms (sod-covered structures, underground food caches) may have different code pathways; coordinate with local tribal building authorities when applicable

### Utility Systems in Cold Climates

- **Piped water and sewer**: Available in Anchorage, Fairbanks, and larger communities; not available in most rural villages
- **Utilidors**: Insulated utility corridors (above-grade or below-grade) carry water and sewer where in-ground pipes would freeze; standard infrastructure in arctic and subarctic villages
- **Honeybucket or vault privy**: Permitted in remote areas without piped sewer service under state regulations
- **Fuel oil storage**: Heating oil is the primary fuel across most of rural Alaska; aboveground storage tanks (AST) require secondary containment and spill prevention plan per ADEC regulations
- **Generator power**: Primary or backup power in off-grid communities; fuel storage and spill prevention design required

### Heating Systems

- **Fuel oil / heating oil**: Most common fuel in rural Alaska; boiler/radiator (hydronic) or forced-air oil furnace systems
- **Natural gas**: Available in Anchorage, Fairbanks area, and some communities along pipeline corridors
- **Propane**: Rural communities without natural gas; special storage design considerations at cold temperatures (propane vapor pressure drops at extreme cold)
- **Biomass and wood**: Significant heat source in SE Alaska; firebox and masonry chimney design per IRC Chapters 10 and 18
- **Heat pumps**: Increasingly viable in Anchorage and SE AK as technology improves; cold-climate heat pumps effective to approximately -15F; not practical in Fairbanks or Zone 8 at typical design temperatures with current technology
- **Combustion air**: Critical in tight envelopes; sealed combustion (direct vent) appliances required wherever natural ventilation openings would undermine energy performance or cause depressurization

### Radon

- Most of Alaska is EPA Radon Zone 1 (highest risk category; predicted average indoor levels greater than 4 pCi/L)
- Fairbanks area has particularly elevated radon levels associated with granitic bedrock uranium content
- Radon-resistant new construction (RRNC) on every below-grade or slab-on-grade residential project:
  - 4" gravel gas-permeable sub-slab layer
  - 6-mil polyethylene vapor barrier on sub-slab gravel
  - All sub-slab penetrations sealed
  - 3" or 4" PVC passive vent pipe from sub-slab to above roof
  - Electrical junction box in attic near vent for future active fan installation
- Post-construction radon test before occupancy; active fan if results exceed 4 pCi/L

### Corrosion in Coastal Areas

- Salt air accelerates corrosion of steel connectors, fasteners, and MEP components in all coastal locations
- Specify stainless steel, hot-dipped galvanized (minimum HDG), or polymer-coated fasteners and hardware for all exterior and exposed applications
- HVAC equipment: coastal-rated units with epoxy-coated or copper-fin coils

## Notable State Amendments

1. IBC 2018 base: Alaska adopts IBC 2018 with amendments; Municipality of Anchorage and Fairbanks North Star Borough have substantial additional local amendments; always verify with the local AHJ
2. Municipality of Anchorage (MOA): has its own building department with significant local amendments; governs the majority of the state's population; MOA amendments may differ from state code on important matters
3. Fairbanks North Star Borough (FNSB): independent building authority with permafrost-specific requirements and local amendments to address Interior Alaska conditions
4. No statewide residential sprinkler mandate: sprinklers strongly encouraged in areas with long fire response times; some Anchorage subdivisions may have local requirements
5. Seismic engineering required: IRC prescriptive tables and methods do not apply in SDC D-E; a structural engineer familiar with Alaska seismic conditions is required for residential design across most of the state
6. Permafrost geotechnical investigation: required for any structure on discontinuous or continuous permafrost; there is no prescriptive foundation table substitute
7. AHFC energy standards: Alaska Housing Finance Corporation ratings affect financing eligibility, weatherization program access, and grant eligibility; many projects are designed to AHFC criteria rather than IECC minimum
8. Remote village code compliance: unincorporated villages may have limited or no local building code enforcement capacity; the state minimum code applies by law but field enforcement capacity varies significantly

## Quick Checklist - New Residential

- [ ] Confirm climate zone (7 or 8) for project location
- [ ] IECC 2018 Zone 7/8 envelope values applied - ceiling R-49, walls with continuous insulation, floor R-38, slab R-15 for 4 ft, windows U-0.28 or better
- [ ] Triple-pane windows specified (Zone 8 / Fairbanks and colder locations)
- [ ] HRV or ERV mechanical ventilation system designed and scheduled
- [ ] Vapor retarder strategy confirmed - Class I poly on warm-in-winter side; no vapor traps between two impermeable layers
- [ ] Air barrier continuity detailed throughout envelope; all penetrations sealed
- [ ] Seismic design category (SDC) determined from USGS hazard maps and site-specific geologic setting
- [ ] SDC D or E confirmed: structural engineer engaged; IRC prescriptive tables not applicable
- [ ] Geotechnical investigation ordered: permafrost characterization, frost depth, liquefaction and landslide assessment as applicable
- [ ] Foundation system selected based on permafrost status or frost depth (Anchorage minimum: 48 inches)
- [ ] Permafrost present: open pile foundation or ventilated crawlspace designed to preserve frozen ground
- [ ] Permafrost present: thermosyphons designed if required by geotechnical engineer
- [ ] Snow load confirmed per ASCE 7 and local data; site-specific snow study if in extreme snowfall area (Valdez, passes)
- [ ] Drift and sliding snow loads evaluated; slide zone managed at grade level
- [ ] Avalanche hazard assessment completed for mountain or coastal terrain
- [ ] Wind design per ASCE 7; Williwaw risk evaluated for susceptible locations
- [ ] Coastal corrosion: stainless or HDG fasteners and hardware specified
- [ ] Tsunami inundation map reviewed for coastal sites (NOAA / DCRA maps)
- [ ] Volcanic ash fall: HVAC filter access and ash load evaluated for Cook Inlet area
- [ ] Radon-resistant construction designed: gravel, vapor barrier, vent pipe, attic outlet rough-in
- [ ] Heating system sized for local design temperature (as low as -47F for Fairbanks)
- [ ] Combustion air: sealed combustion direct-vent appliances specified
- [ ] Water and sewer: piped system, utilidor, or approved vault privy system confirmed
- [ ] Fuel oil AST: secondary containment and ADEC spill prevention plan designed
- [ ] Smoke and CO alarms per IRC 2018
- [ ] Egress windows per IRC R310 in all sleeping rooms
- [ ] Verify local AHJ (MOA, FNSB, or state Division of Fire and Life Safety) for adopted code edition and amendments
